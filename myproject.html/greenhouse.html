Greenhouse Effect

Introduction to Green house

A greenhouse is a structure designed to create and maintain a controlled environment for the cultivation of plants.
It is primarily used for gardening and agricultural purposes, allowing for the cultivation of a wide range of plants, including vegetables, flowers, and ornamental plants, in a controlled setting.
Greenhouses are built with transparent or translucent walls and roofs, typically made of materials like glass or plastic, to allow sunlight to enter while trapping heat.
This greenhouse effect, where sunlight is absorbed and re-radiated as heat within the structure, helps maintain a higher temperature and humidity level than the external environment.

Advantages of green house

  1. Extended Growing Season: Greenhouses provide a stable and warmer environment, allowing plants to be grown year-round, even in regions with harsh winters.
  2. Temperature Control: The temperature inside a greenhouse can be regulated to optimize plant growth, as different plants have specific temperature requirements.
  3. Protection from Weather: Greenhouses shield plants from harsh weather conditions like strong winds, heavy rains, and hail, reducing the risk of damage.
  4. Pest and Disease Control: The controlled environment can help keep pests and diseases at bay, reducing the need for chemical treatments.
  5. Increased Crop Yield: With optimal conditions, plants in greenhouses tend to grow faster and produce higher yields compared to open-field cultivation.

It is important that we take steps to reduce our greenhouse gas emissions and mitigate the effects of global warming. Sources of green house

Greenhouse gases are gases in the Earth's atmosphere that can trap heat, leading to the greenhouse effect and contributing to global warming and climate change. The primary sources of greenhouse gases include:

Methane (CH4):
  1. Agriculture: Enteric fermentation in livestock, especially cattle, and rice cultivation.
  2. Natural gas production and distribution.
  3. Landfills and waste management.
  4. Wetlands and natural sources.
Nitrous Oxide (N2O):
  1. Agricultural activities, particularly the use of synthetic fertilizers.
  2. Industrial processes and the combustion of fossil fuels.
  3. Biomass burning.
